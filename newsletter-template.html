Bitcoin Dips 0.78% – Here's Why the Quiet Matters More Than the Drop

Bitcoin dropped from $62,100 to $61,500 overnight. On the surface: not much. But the volume of trades fell too, which tells a different story. When price moves on low volume, it means fewer people are pushing the button. That's a sign of uncertainty.
Usually when Bitcoin sells off hard, the news is dramatic. A regulation threat. A hack. A celebrity meltdown. This time? No single story explains it. A few traders took profits. Some bots rebalanced. A small amount of liquidations rippled through.
Here's the thing: stability in crypto is still unusual. Bitcoin spent years being wildly volatile. Now when it moves 0.78%, people notice. That's actually progress. It means we're treating it less like a lottery ticket and more like an asset that people hold long-term.

Thailand Just Made Bitcoin Official. Here's What That Actually Means.

Thailand's central bank published new guidance this week. Bitcoin and crypto now have an official regulatory framework. They're not banning it. They're not promoting it. They're acknowledging it exists and writing rules around it. This is surprisingly rare for Southeast Asia.
What does "framework" mean in practice? Banks can now legally serve crypto customers. Exchanges need licenses. Users who trade crypto have to verify their identity (know-your-customer rules). It's basically the same playbook El Salvador used, but more cautious. Thailand is saying: we'll allow this, but we're watching.
Why does this matter beyond Thailand? Because Southeast Asia is young, growing, and still deciding how to treat crypto. When a major economy like Thailand moves, others watch. If Thailand's framework works—if crime doesn't spike, if people don't lose their life savings to scams—other countries will copy it. If it goes wrong, they'll reject it entirely.

The Man Who Owns More Bitcoin Than Most Countries. Yes, Really.

Mike Novogratz is a hedge fund manager who owns approximately 145,000 Bitcoin. At today's price, that's roughly $8.9 billion worth. To put that in perspective: fewer than 2 million people on Earth own any Bitcoin at all. This one person owns enough to rank in the top 0.0001%.
He didn't inherit it. He built a crypto hedge fund in 2018 when Bitcoin was crashing. He bought when everyone else was selling. He's been clear about this: he thinks Bitcoin is going higher, possibly much higher. So he holds. In finance, that's called conviction. In reality, it's a bet.
Here's what's interesting: a single person now controls a meaningful amount of the world's Bitcoin supply. Not enough to crash the market alone, but enough to matter. When he speaks, markets move. When he buys, volume spikes. Does that bother you? It bothers some people. Others see it as proof that Bitcoin works—if you believed early and stuck with it, you get rewarded.

What Is a Bitcoin Wallet? (And Why the Name Is Slightly Misleading)

Here's the trap: a Bitcoin wallet doesn't actually hold Bitcoin. It holds keys. Specifically, it holds a "private key"—a long string of characters that proves you own your Bitcoin. The Bitcoin itself lives on the blockchain (a public ledger). Your wallet is just the thing that unlocks it, like a password.
Think of it this way. A Bitcoin address is your account number. Anyone can send Bitcoin to your address. Your private key is your password. Never share it. If someone gets your private key, they own your Bitcoin. Not because they know where it is—because they can prove they're you.
There are different types of wallets: apps on your phone (hot wallets), hardware devices like USB sticks (cold wallets), or even just written down on paper (paper wallets). Hot wallets are convenient but less secure. Cold wallets are secure but less convenient. Most people use both: small amounts in apps, large amounts in cold storage.

We Tested Coinbase for Beginners. Here Is the Honest Version.

Our team signed up for Coinbase as complete newbies. No crypto experience. Just three questions: Is it obvious what to do? Is it actually secure? Will you charge me to death? Verdict: two out of three, which is better than most financial apps.
The good: verification takes 5 minutes. Buying Bitcoin is straightforward—pick an amount, confirm, done. The interface is clean. You can see your Bitcoin in real-time. The security features are solid (two-factor authentication, email confirmations). Coinbase has been around since 2012 and it hasn't been hacked.
The bad: their fees are higher than other exchanges. If you buy $100 of Bitcoin, Coinbase takes about $1.49 in fees. Smaller exchanges charge less. Also, you can't export your Bitcoin to a private wallet without jumping through hoops. They want to keep you on their platform.
